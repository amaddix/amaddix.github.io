ABOUT ME

RESUME


I didn't walk into my computer science degree with complete certainty — I walked in curious, and a little nervous. What I found on the other side completely changed me.

From the moment I started building, I knew this was it. There's nothing quite like starting from a blank file and crafting something that actually works — designing the structure, breaking everything down to its smallest moving parts, finding the cleanest way to organize it all. It feels like solving a puzzle. A puzzle that, without fail, breaks on you.

Sure, a stubborn bug at midnight can make me want to lose my mind. But tracking it down — figuring out exactly what went wrong and why — is genuinely one of the most satisfying feelings I know. That obsession with getting every little piece interacting perfectly is what keeps me locked in, and always improving my work.

I consider myself a fullstack developer with a natural pull toward the backend. I love architecting the core of a system — thinking through the logic, the data flow, the foundation that everything else depends on. But I also care deeply about aesthetics and cohesion in my everyday life, and that sensibility carries straight into my frontend work. Because at the end of the day, I believe a backend is only as good as the cohesive, polished experience it provides the user. Clean design isn't an afterthought to me — it's part of the craft.


Outside of software, I'm always chasing the next challenge. I travel as much as I can, drawn to places with striking geology and rich cultural or historical roots — the kind of places that make you feel just how vast and layered the world really is. I love hiking and don’t shy away from the brutal ones — Angels Landing in Zion, Grinnell Glacier in Glacier National Park, and Dog Mountain along the Columbia River Gorge have all humbled me in the best way. And yes, I'm absolutely the person stopping mid-trail to look at every bird and bug I come across — I find wonder in the details everywhere I look, and that curiosity never really turns off.
